4. Recommended Legal Strategies

Strategy 1: Modifier Approach (PRIMARY - LOWEST COST)

What it is: File marks as compound marks with "FEEL GOOD TONIC" suffix, exactly matching our actual can labeling.

Example: Instead of fighting for "FLOW," register "FLOW LEMON ELDERFLOWER FEEL GOOD TONIC"

Why it works:
  • Compound marks create distinct commercial impression from standalone words
  • Matches our actual product labeling (no specimen issues)
  • We still preserve common law rights to use "FLOW" alone in commerce
  • Much higher approval rate than fighting for generic single-word marks

Cost: ~$400/mark (new filing) vs. $8,500/mark (fighting refusal)
Success Rate: ~75%

Strategy 2: Crowded Field Doctrine

What it is: Demonstrate that terms like FLOW, MUSE are widely used across beverage categories, making them "weak marks" entitled only to narrow protection.

Legal Basis: DuPont Factor 6 - Per Juice Generation v. GS Enterprises (Fed. Cir. 2015): Third-party use evidence proves consumers distinguish between similar marks in crowded fields.

How to execute:
  • Compile USPTO registrations containing FLOW, MUSE across beverage/food categories
  • Document actual marketplace use (websites, Amazon, retail photos)
  • Submit as evidence that these are common, weak formatives

Cost: ~$2,000/mark | Success Rate: ~65%

Strategy 3: Narrow Goods Description

What it is: Amend goods description to be highly specific, avoiding overlap with cited marks.

Current (too broad): "Non-alcoholic beverages"

Recommended language:
"Non-alcoholic beverages, namely, adaptogenic functional tonics containing plant extracts; non-alcoholic herbal beverages infused with adaptogens; non-alcoholic relaxation beverages containing functional ingredients for mood enhancement"

Why it works: Creates clear distinction from cited marks (water, wine, tea, soft drinks). Different trade channels (wellness retail vs. grocery beverage aisle).

Cost: ~$400/mark | Success Rate: ~60%

Strategy 4: Consent/Coexistence Agreement

What it is: Negotiate agreement with blocking mark owner for peaceful coexistence.

When to use: Blocking mark owner is a small company unlikely to compete with adaptogenic tonics.

Requirements for USPTO to accept:
  • NOT a "naked consent" - must explain WHY confusion is unlikely
  • Identify different trade channels, consumer demographics
  • Include enforceable steps to prevent confusion
  • Geographic or product category limitations

Cost: ~$2,500-$4,000/agreement | Success Rate: ~55%

9. Why This Strategy Will Work

The Core Argument

Other brands get trademarks approved for similar generic words because they:
  1. File compound marks with descriptive modifiers (LaCroix + flavor names)
  2. Use highly specific goods descriptions that don't overlap with existing marks
  3. Argue "crowded field" when the term is commonly used across categories
  4. Negotiate consent agreements with blocking mark owners

We haven't been doing this. We've been filing single generic words ("FLOW," "MUSE") with broad goods descriptions ("non-alcoholic beverages").

The Compelling Case for Myers Law Group

Why this path is worth the investment:
  1. Matches reality: Our cans already say "[Flavor] Feel Good Tonic" - we're just registering what we actually use
  2. 85%+ success rate on non-THC evidence submissions - low-hanging fruit
  3. 75% success rate on compound mark filings vs. 35% fighting single-word refusals
  4. $87,100 in savings compared to fighting everything individually
  5. Common law rights preserved: We can still use "FLOW" alone, we just can't stop others from using it for different products (which we don't care about anyway)
  6. Our actual goal: Prevent other functional beverage companies from using our marks - this strategy achieves that

Addressing the "Weaker Mark" Concern

Concern: "If we add a modifier, doesn't that weaken our trademark?"

Reality:
  • A registered compound mark is STRONGER than an unregistered single word
  • We keep common law rights to the single word through actual use
  • Our actual concern is functional beverage competitors - the compound mark protects against that
  • We don't care if Flow Water or Muse Wine exists - they're not our competitors
  • A narrow-scope registered mark IS protection for our specific category
